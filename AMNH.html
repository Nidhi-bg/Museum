American Museum of Natural History


Overview

The American Museum of Natural History is a grand monolith from the late 19th century that spans four city blocks on the Upper West Side, just across from Central Park. When visitors choose only one museum in NYC, it's likely either this one or the Met.

All aspects of the natural world are represented here, from a vast collection of taxidermy mammals (many donated by Theodore Roosevelt, from his African safaris), to depictions of the life of Native American tribes, to an entire hall dedicated to marine life—including a life-size model of a blue whale. The crown jewel for many, though, is the dinosaur floor, with an imposing skeleton of a Tyrannosaurus Rex that dominates the room.

There are usually two special exhibits at any time, including recent shows like an in-depth, immersive exhibit dealing with human senses and perception and a deep-dive into the world of mummies, with examples of ancient Egyptians and Peruvians preserved for thousands of years.

  • Established in: April 6, 1869; 154 years ago
  • Location: Upper West Side of Manhattan, 200 Central Park West, New York, NY 10024
  • Collection size: 35 million specimens
  • Hours:
    • Weekdays: 10:00 AM to 5:30 PM
    • Weekends: 10:00 AM to 5:30 PM
  • Director: Lisa Gugenheim

My Experience

Walking into the American Museum of Natural History was like stepping into a world where history, science, and art converged. The iconic place I had seen in movies now felt real, and the excitement was palpable.

One exhibit that stood out was the colossal skeleton of a Tyrannosaurus rex. It was a moment of déjà vu as I recalled how this iconic dinosaur had come to life on the big screen. Seeing the museum featured in movies like "Night at the Museum." It felt like I was stepping into a familiar world filled with both wonder and nostalgia.

But what really struck me was seeing the "Lucy" exhibit for the first time. "Lucy" is like an ancient family member, one of our earliest human ancestors. It made me think about how we humans have come a long way and yet are part of a much larger story. It was a very humbling experience that filled me with a deep appreciation for the incredible diversity of life on Earth.

Lucy's skeleton is considered one of the most complete early hominid fossils ever found. It provided valuable insights into the evolution of bipedalism (walking on two legs) in our ancestors. Lucy had a small brain relative to modern humans but walked upright, which was a critical evolutionary development. Her fossilized bones revealed a combination of ape-like and human-like features, offering a glimpse into the transitional stage of hominid evolution. The discovery of Lucy's remains revolutionized our understanding of human evolution and our connections to earlier species. It provided strong evidence for the theory that early hominids evolved in Africa and were our direct ancestors.

The museum didn't just bring movie scenes to life; it opened my eyes to new and incredible exhibits that left a mark on my understanding of our world and where we fit into the grand scheme of things.
